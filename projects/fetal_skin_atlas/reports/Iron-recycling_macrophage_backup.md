# Iron-recycling Macrophage  
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: fetal

## Summary

Iron-recycling macrophages are a distinct subpopulation of fetal skin macrophages identified in the recent prenatal skin atlas (Gopee et al., 2024). These cells are defined by gene expression programs that support iron metabolism and vascular development. Functionally, fetal skin iron-recycling macrophages are implicated in supporting endothelial cell chemotaxis and angiogenesis, consistent with specialized roles for macrophages in development beyond immunity. While classical tissue locations for iron-recycling macrophages include the spleen and liver, this cell population is now confirmed in prenatal human skin. Their key functional attribute—efficient recycling of iron via phagocytosis and metabolism of senescent erythrocytes—is highly conserved from fetal to adult life (Webb and Haniffa, 2023).

## Markers

No specific marker genes for iron-recycling macrophages in prenatal human skin were provided in the traversed literature or supplementary materials. However, mechanistic discussion in the broader literature associates markers such as NRAMP1/DMT1, HRG1, and ferroportin (FPN1) with the iron import and export functions of iron-recycling macrophages (González-Domínguez et al., 2020; Mazgaj et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023).

- **NRAMP1/DMT1**: Involved in ferrous iron uptake. This transporter allows macrophages to import iron for subsequent processing.
- **HRG1**: Transports heme from fully degraded erythrocytes across the phagosome membrane into the cytosol.
- **Ferroportin (FPN1)**: The only known iron exporter in macrophages, critical for releasing iron back to the circulation.

Additionally, these macrophages are functionally characterized by their capacity to phagocytose erythrocytes through recognition of signals such as phosphatidylserine exposure and loss of CD47 on aging red blood cells (González-Domínguez et al., 2020).

## Location

### Prenatal Human Skin

The identity of "Iron-recycling macrophage" in prenatal (fetal) human skin is explicitly recognized and annotated in the prenatal skin atlas, using single-cell transcriptomic approaches (Gopee et al., 2024). The atlas confirms their presence as a subcluster of macrophages in developing skin.

### Splenic and Hepatic Niches

While most prior research has focused on the spleen and liver as the main tissue sites for iron-recycling macrophages (where they are involved in bulk erythrophagocytosis), the current atlas demonstrates their presence in the skin during fetal development. The broader literature consistently highlights the spleen and liver as principal sites:

> "This population includes macrophages primarily from the spleen and liver that carry out iron-recycling functions (21)."
>
> — Suo et al. (2022)

### Conservation Across Development

Iron-recycling macrophages retain a highly similar molecular and functional profile from fetal into adult life, in contrast to other prenatal macrophage types:

> "Analysis of myeloid cells in the fetal spleen suggested that iron-recycling erythrophagocytic macrophages might have conserved features with their adult counterparts (unlike other prenatal macrophages), indicating that this iron-recycling profile is retained with great fidelity from fetal into adult life."
>
> — Webb and Haniffa (2023)

## Function

### Iron Homeostasis and Recycling

Iron-recycling macrophages are central to systemic iron balance. They ingest and degrade senescent erythrocytes, liberate heme, and process the released iron for export:

> "macrophages are deeply involved in iron recycling in various ways."
>
> — González-Domínguez et al. (2020)

Mechanistically, iron-recycling is achieved via multiple pathways:

> "Macrophages present different iron uptake mechanisms such as the usual import of transferrin-bound or non-transferrin-bound iron, ferrous iron uptake via natural resistance-associated macrophage protein 1 (NRAMP1) or Dmt1 (also known as NRAMP2, as mentioned above) or senescent erythrocyte phagocytosis [95,96]."
>
> — González-Domínguez et al. (2020)

The recognition and engulfment of aged red blood cells relies on specific surface alterations:

> "To accomplish phagocytosis, macrophages recognize some ageing signals exhibited by senescent erythrocytes, such as an increase of membrane phosphatidylserine and a decrease in membrane flexibility or the antigen CD47 [97]."
>
> — González-Domínguez et al. (2020)

After erythrocyte uptake:

> "Erythrocytes are then incorporated into the denominated phagolysosomes, where they are degraded by ROS and hydrolytic enzymes until heme release."
>
> — González-Domínguez et al. (2020)

Subsequent processing involves heme breakdown:

> "Released heme is degraded by the above-mentioned HO-1 into biliverdin, carbon monoxide and ferrous iron."
>
> — González-Domínguez et al. (2020)

And stepwise intracellular routing of iron:

> "Iron transport within the macrophage is performed in three stages. First, heme is transported to the cytosol across phagosome membrane by heme responsive gene 1 (HRG1). After its degradation, ferrous iron is transported through the cytoplasm by PCBP1, and is finally delivered to FPN1 to be exported and reduced by CP in circulation, making possible its transport through the organism via Tf [76,95,96]."
>
> — González-Domínguez et al. (2020)

### Prenatal Skin Vascular Development

Iron-recycling macrophages in fetal human skin contribute to blood vessel formation by promoting the migration of endothelial cells—an angiogenic function distinct from their classical homeostatic roles:

> "Gene ontology analysis showed that the four macrophage subsets (LYVE1 + , MHCII + , TML and iron-recycling) expressed gene programs that drive angiogenesis (Supplementary Tables 23-26)."
>
> — Gopee et al. (2024)

Specifically, iron-recycling macrophages showed unique transcriptional signatures linked to the recruitment of endothelial cells:

> "Gene module expression profiles suggested that sprouting angiogenesis (growth of new vessels) was promoted by LYVE1 + and TML macrophages, blood vessel morphogenesis by LYVE1 + macrophages and endothelial cell chemotaxis by iron-recycling macrophages (Extended Data Fig. 8i and Supplementary Table 27)."
>
> — Gopee et al. (2024)

### Maintenance of Iron Supply During Pregnancy

The capacity of iron-recycling macrophages to export iron is critical, especially during states of increased demand or deficiency, such as in pregnancy:

> "it is mainly expressed in liver macrophages (Browicz-Kupffer cells), and this expression is only slightly decreased by an iron-deficient diet. This may be due to the specificity of iron metabolism in Browicz-Kupffer cells, which play an essential role in iron recycling through the phagocytosis of senescent RBCs, and thus continuously acquire iron in large amounts."
>
> — Mazgaj et al. (2021)

Similarly, splenic macrophages are highlighted:

> "A large amount of iron is supplied by iron recycling of macrophages in the spleen where macrophages export Fe 2+ into plasma by ferroportin (Fpn1 or Slc40a1) after phagocytosis of senescent erythrocytes and breakdown of hemoglobins (22)."
>
> — Li et al. (2023)

And their role in systemic iron distribution is further underscored:

> "Blocking the iron exporter of macrophages could reduce the serum iron level and the number of RBCs in mice (24)."
>
> — Li et al. (2023)
