# Melanocyte (C_Melanocyte)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

Melanocytes are pigment-producing cells found predominantly in the basal layer of the epidermis and in the appendages of the skin, such as hair follicles and, to a lesser extent, glands. Identified by markers such as MLANA, PMEL, TYR, and DCT, melanocytes are specialized for the synthesis of melanin, the pigment responsible for skin, hair, and eye coloration. Functionally, they play a major role in protecting the skin from ultraviolet (UV) radiation by transferring melanosomes to keratinocytes. Melanocytes exhibit distinct structural features, including dendritic morphology, and exist in heterogeneous populations with varying differentiation states.

## Markers

Extensive evidence supports several markers specific to melanocytes, which are functionally linked to melanogenesis, cell survival, and differentiation:

- **MLANA (Melan-A/MART1)**: This is a widely used marker for identifying melanocytes and is involved in melanosome formation (Table S3, Cui et al., 2023).
- **PMEL (Pmel17/gp100)**: Crucial for the structural maturation of melanosomes, PMEL serves as a reliable melanocyte marker (Table S3, Cui et al., 2023).
- **TYR (Tyrosinase)**: Tyrosinase is an enzyme essential for melanin biosynthesis and distinguishes differentiated melanocytes (Table S3; Kormos et al., 2011; Song et al., 2025).
- **DCT (Dopachrome tautomerase/TYRP2)**: Functions in melanin synthesis and is expressed in cells at various melanogenic states, also marking lineage commitment (Table S3, Cui et al., 2023).
- **MITF (Microphthalmia-associated transcription factor)**: A master regulator of melanocyte differentiation and melanin enzyme expression (Song et al., 2025; Casalou et al., 2023).
- **c-KIT (CD117)**: This receptor tyrosine kinase marks both precursor and some mature melanocyte populations and is essential for migration and survival (Casalou et al., 2023; Wong et al., 2006; Cichorek et al., 2013).
- **SOX10**: Marks early melanocyte differentiation and neural crest lineage (Casalou et al., 2023; Cichorek et al., 2013).
- **S100**: Used for identification in human tissues, although not exclusive for melanocytes (Jang et al., 2014).
- **TRP-1 (TYRP1), TRP-2 (DCT)**: Involved in melanogenesis and used as markers of both mature and some precursor melanocytes (Kormos et al., 2011; Casalou et al., 2023; Song et al., 2025).

These markers were corroborated both by supplemental annotation in the skin atlas and by multiple independent studies.

## Location

### Epidermis

Melanocytes are commonly situated in the basal epidermal layer:

> "Melanocytes comprise from 5% to 10% of the cells in the basal layer of epidermis. Epidermal melanocytes are thin, elongated cell with branched structures, consisting of a central cell body and long numerous branches, or dendrites through which they interact with the adjacent keratinocytes of the basal epidermal layer."
>
> — Ali et al. (2015)

Supplementary data from the referenced skin atlas indicated melanocytes were specifically identified in adult skin samples from the epidermal layers (Table S3).

> "Melanocytes, referred to as pigment cells, are abundant within the human epidermis, with an approximate of 1,500 cells per square millimetre (Kanitakis, 2002). These pigmented cells are generally located in the dermis or epidermis and play a crucial role in skin pigmentation."
>
> — Cui et al. (2023)

### Skin Appendages

Melanocytes are also distributed in hair follicles, and to a lesser extent, sebaceous and sweat glands:

> "a considerable fraction of cutaneous melanocytes reside outside of the epidermis in adult human skin; mostly located in multiple distinct compartments of the hair follicle, its associated sebaceous gland, and even in sweat glands [see Figure 3;(73)]."
>
> — Casalou et al. (2022)

> "Melanocyte stem cells reside in the hair follicle bulge, located in the outer root sheath 2 ."
>
> — Jang et al. (2014)

### Developmental Context

During embryogenesis, melanocytes originate from neural crest-derived melanoblasts that migrate through the dermis to reach their final destinations:

> "neural crestderived melanoblasts (Mb) migrate into the developing dermis (blue), and either differentiate into dermal melanocytes (Mc,1) or continue migrating into the epidermis (pink, 2)."
>
> — Fitch et al. (2003)

## Function

### Melanin Synthesis and Pigment Transfer

Melanocytes are specialized for melanin biosynthesis, which is subsequently transferred to keratinocytes:

> "In the human epidermis, melanocytes produce unique subcellular lysosome-related organelles called melanosomes that are responsible for melanogenesis (24,25)."
>
> — Casalou et al. (2022)

> "These cytoplasmic connections provide conduits for the transfer of pigment granules (and likely other melanocytic cellular material) to both basal and suprabasal keratinocytes (22,24,25)."
>
> — Casalou et al. (2022)

This transfer of melanin granules forms the basis of the epidermal melanin unit:

> "Melanocytes and keratinocytes exist in the human epidermis within a truly extraordinary and remarkably-stable unit of one post-mitotic melanocyte to approximately 36 viable keratinocytes -the so-called 'epidermal melanin unit' or 'EMU,' as first proposed by Breathnach and Fitzpatrick in 1963 (27). This unit is crucial for maintaining the integrity of the human epidermis and especially for its protective skin pigmentation (see Figure 2)."
>
> — Casalou et al. (2022)

### UV Protection

Melanin produced by melanocytes serves to shield underlying keratinocytes from ultraviolet radiation:

> "melanocyte 'donations' to keratinocytes influences their physiology, not least by protecting this critical proliferative cell population from UVR damage (39)."
>
> — Casalou et al. (2022)

> "Melanin in the epidermis participate in ray protection."
>
> — Chen et al. (2021)

### Maintenance of Homeostasis and Regeneration

Melanocyte stem cells in hair follicles are a reservoir for regeneration, particularly following skin injury or UV stimulation:

> "melanocyte stem cells provide mature melanocytes to the hair bulb to produce pigment for the newly formed hair follicles, as well as supplying mature melanocytes to the interfollicular epidermis, serving as a reservoir for mature melanocytes in adult skin [13]."
>
> — Shen et al. (2024)

## Structure / Morphology

Melanocytes display characteristic ultrastructural and morphological features:

> "Epidermal melanocytes are thin, elongated cell with branched structures, consisting of a central cell body and long numerous branches, or dendrites through which they interact with the adjacent keratinocytes of the basal epidermal layer [26]."
>
> — Ali et al. (2015)

> "Melanocytes were identified by the presence of melanosomes in varying stages of maturity in the cytoplasm, and by lack of tonofilaments or desmosomes, well-known ultrastructural features of normal melanocytes in the basal layer that permit them to be easily distinguished from keratinocytes (44)."
>
> — Hara et al. (1996)

> "Differentiated melanocytes are characterized by melanin production due to the activities of tyrosinase, TRP-1 and TRP-2; by numerous mature melanosomes and well-developed dendrites [3]."
>
> — Kormos et al. (2011)

These dendritic extensions facilitate melanin transfer and cell–cell interaction in the epidermis.
