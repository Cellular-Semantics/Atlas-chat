# DC1
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: adult

## Summary

DC1, also known as type 1 conventional dendritic cells, represent a key antigen-presenting subset found in adult and fetal human skin, closely resembling their adult counterparts even during prenatal development (Botting et al., 2020). DC1 is defined by high expression of markers such as XCR1, CLEC9A, and CD141 (BDCA-3) in humans and CD103 in mice. Functionally, DC1 specializes in cross-presenting antigen to CD8+ T cells, promoting Th1 polarization, and exerting important roles in both innate and adaptive immune responses, including host defense, regulation of T cell responses, and potential contributions to immune tolerance and homeostasis (Vobořil et al., 2025; Lee et al., 2013). Evidence highlights their relevance in the skin and other tissues, where they mediate both proinflammatory and tolerogenic effects depending on context.

## Markers

DC1 cells can be rigorously identified by several marker genes and surface proteins, which are functionally relevant for their role in immunity:

- **XCR1**: A chemokine receptor uniquely expressed on DC1 cells. It is used to precisely deplete or target DC1 populations in murine models and serves as a defining feature distinguishing these cells from other dendritic cell subsets.  
  > "Using XCR1-DTR mice, we were able to precisely target and deplete only DC1, because XCR1 expression is limited to DC1 alone."  
  > — Moorman et al. (2023)

- **CLEC9A (DNGR1)**: A C-type lectin receptor engaged in sensing necrotic cells and facilitating cross-presentation, critical for initiating CD8+ T cell responses (Vobořil et al., 2025).

- **CD141 (BDCA-3)**: In humans, DC1 are identified as CD141+ classical dendritic cells, corresponding functionally to CD8α+ DCs in mice.  
  > "Comparable to murine CD8a + and CD11b + DC subsets, human classical DCs can be separated into CD141 + (DC1) and CD1c + (DC2) subsets, respectively (24, 25)."  
  > — Andreas et al. (2023)

- **CD103**: Expressed in non-lymphoid tissues in mice, CD103+ DC1 correspond to human CD141+ DC1 and are associated with tissue residency and migratory capacity (Tartour & Johannes, 2022).

- **Flt3 (FLT3-L dependent)**: Fms-like tyrosine kinase 3 is essential for their differentiation and homeostasis (Cuzzubbo et al., 2022).

- **Other markers**: CD205, CD207, CD24, and, contextually, Cd209a and MHCII (Vobořil et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2021).

- **CD275 (ICOS-L), CCR7, CD15, CD56**: These markers are upregulated in DC1 under conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis and indicate an activated or migratory phenotype.  
  > "DC1 (CD141+: Supplementary Figure S12) showed significant increases in CD275 (ICOS-L) in RA ... CCR7 was also higher compared to control DC1 ... We also observed higher CD15 in RA DC1 (CD141+); CD56 was numerically higher but not statistically different ..."  
  > — Geier et al. (2025)

## Location

### Skin (Adult and Developmental)

- **Adult and Fetal Skin**  
  DC1 is a constituent of the dermal antigen-presenting cell network in both adult and first-trimester human skin, where it appears early during development and closely resembles the adult phenotype:
  > "Mature adult skin contains the dendritic cell (DC) subsets DC1 and DC2, as well as macrophages, all of which have been identified in first-trimester human skin and closely resemble their adult counterparts."
  >
  > — Botting et al. (2020)

### Other Tissues

- **Peripheral Blood & Lymphoid Tissues**  
  DC1 are present in various tissues, including peripheral blood, lymph nodes, thymus, and organs such as the lung and intestine (Vobořil et al., 2025; Andreas et al., 2023).

- **Thymic Localization**  
  DC1 develop intrathymically and are predominantly localized to the medullary region:
  > "Pre-DC1 cells enter the thymus during embryonic development in a CCR7-dependent manner, giving rise to fully committed DC1 cells that predominantly localize to the thymic medullary region [82]."
  >
  > — Vobořil et al. (2025)

- **In Disease Contexts**  
  DC1 frequency may be altered in the tumor microenvironment (e.g., increased in lung cancer tissue and decreased in peripheral blood in NSCLC patients; Yang et al., 2018).

## Function

### Antigen Presentation and T Cell Priming

- **Cross-Presentation**  
  DC1 are highly specialized in presenting exogenous and endogenous antigens to CD8+ T cells:
  > "Functionally, DC1 are specialized in cross-presenting antigens to CD8 + T cells and in promoting Th1 differentiation [66]."
  >
  > — Vobořil et al. (2025)

- **Priming of Cytotoxic and Th1 Responses**  
  > "they play a critical role in priming CD8 + T cells during antiviral and antitumor immune responses and are an essential part of effective responses to checkpoint blockade therapy [73]."
  >
  > — Vobořil et al. (2025)

  > "DC1 play a critical role in presenting endogenous autoantigens to activate pathogenic T cells under homeostatic conditions where there is a lack of tissue damage and proinflammatory cues."
  >
  > — Moorman et al. (2023)

- **Uptake and Presentation of Nanocarriers**  
  DC1 can efficiently uptake and likely present nanoparticles and associated antigen to naive T cells, participating in durable vaccine responses:
  > "both human DC subsets, CD141 + DC1 and CD1c + DC2, were capable of efficiently taking up ACM-Rhodamine, as indicated by the high expression of Rhodamine in both populations, relative to the control (Figure 2e)."
  >
  > — Lam et al. (2021)

### Regulation of T Cell Subsets and Immune Homeostasis

- **IL-12 Production and Treg Inhibition**  
  DC1 secrete high levels of IL-12, supporting Th1 immunity and suppressing regulatory T cell (Treg) activity:
  > "The DC1 population secretes a large amount of IL-12 which is principally involved in Th1 immunity [18]"
  >
  > — Lee et al. (2013)

  > "DC1 are a significant source of IL-12, which suppresses Treg induction, proliferation, and FOXP3 expression levels (54)."
  >
  > — Moorman et al. (2023)

- **Antagonism of Treg Suppression**  
  > "These results collectively demonstrate that DC1 dendritic cells inhibit T reg -mediated suppression of both CD4 and CD8-positive T cells responding to anti-CD3."
  >
  > — Lee et al. (2013)

  > "the DC1 population can inhibit T reg -mediated suppression in cell contact-independent fashion."
  >
  > — Lee et al. (2013)

### Innate Immunity and Host Defense

- **VEGF-α Production and Neutrophil Recruitment**  
  > "conventional DC1 cells in the dermis of mouse and human skin in response to various different bacterial insults produced vascular endothelial growth factor-α (VEGF-α), which was critical for mediating neutrophil recruitment and host defense (Janela et al. 2019)."
  >
  > — Miller et al. (2019)

  > "cDC1s in the skin and potentially at other epithelial sites are essential regulators of neutrophil recruitment in the innate immune response to S. aureus (and other bacteria), providing evidence of a role for DCs beyond classical antigen presentation."
  >
  > — Miller et al. (2019)

### Development and Plasticity

- **Developmental Origin**  
  DC1 develop from bone marrow-derived precursors and require FLT3-L for their lineage commitment and maintenance (Cuzzubbo et al., 2022). Transcription factors such as IRF8 and BATF3 are critical for DC1 differentiation (Vobořil et al., 2025).

- **Plasticity & Stability**  
  IFN-γ induces DC1 marker expression; TNF-α programming during polarization stabilizes the DC1 phenotype, making it resistant to DC2-skewing environments:
  > "these data demonstrate that TNFα stimulation during the initial DC1 activation resulted in a stabilized DC1 phenotype (TNFα-programmed DC1) capable of sustaining a DC1 phenotype in a DC2-skewing environment."
  >
  > — Eastman et al. (2019)

- **Tolerance and Central Tolerance**  
  DC1 contribute to maintaining immune homeostasis and participate in central tolerance in the thymus:
  > "Claudin 1-mediated positioning of DC1 to mTECs is essential for antigen transfer-coupled DC1 maturation and maintenance of central tolerance"
  >
  > — Březina et al. (2025)

  > "DC1 also contribute to maintaining homeostasis in mucosal tissues, such as the lungs and intestine, by sampling microbial antigens and exerting tolerogenic functions [74]."
  >
  > — Vobořil et al. (2025)

### Disease-Associated Observations

- **Tumor and Inflammatory Contexts**  
  DC1 levels can be altered in diseases like non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and rheumatoid arthritis:
  > "The expression of DC1 in peripheral blood in NSCLC patients was significantly decreased compared with that in the normal control group, and the difference was statistically significant (P<0.01)."
  >
  > — Yang et al. (2018)

  > "DC1 (CD141+: Supplementary Figure S12) showed significant increases in CD275 (ICOS-L) in RA (Supplementary Figure S12U; p = 0.0065). CCR7 was also higher compared to control DC1..."  
  > — Geier et al. (2025)
