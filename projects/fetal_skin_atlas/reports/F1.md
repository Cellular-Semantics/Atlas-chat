# Papillary Fibroblast 1 (F1)
Atlas: A prenatal skin atlas reveals immune regulation of human skin morphogenesis (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-024-08002-x)  
Scope: Adult

## Summary

Papillary Fibroblast 1 (F1), also called "Papillary fibroblast 1," is a distinct fibroblast subpopulation in adult human skin, predominantly located in the upper papillary dermis. F1 is characterized by the expression of markers such as COL13A1, OGN, COL18A1, C7, and MMP3, and by high levels of fibroblast activation protein (FAP), but low or absent CD90/THY1 expression (Korosec et al., 2019; Supplementary Table S2). These cells play a critical role in extracellular matrix (ECM) organization, support of hair follicle development, and regulation of epidermal differentiation and wound healing. Evidence suggests that papillary fibroblasts exhibit notable heterogeneity, age-related changes, and unique functional properties compared to other dermal fibroblast subtypes.

## Markers

The Papillary Fibroblast 1 (F1) subpopulation is defined by a specific transcriptomic and surface marker profile:

- **COL13A1, OGN, COL18A1, C7, MMP3**: Identified as top differentially expressed genes for F1 in adult skin, these markers are associated with ECM structural integrity and remodeling (Supplementary Table S2).
- **FAP (Fibroblast Activation Protein) positive, CD90 (THY1) negative**: Surface markers consistently identifying papillary fibroblasts in human dermis. FAP positivity distinguishes papillary fibroblasts from other subpopulations, while the lack of CD90/THY1 further specifies their identity (Korosec et al., 2019; Griffin et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019).

> "the papillary fibroblast can be identified as FAP+ CD90−"
>
> — Salazar et al. (2022)

> "fibroblast activation protein-positive (FAP + ) CD90 − (also known as THY1 -) cells are enriched in the papillary dermis."
>
> — Griffin et al. (2020)

> "papillary fibroblasts are FAP + ; THY1 −"
>
> — Liu et al. (2019)

Other reported markers relevant especially in developmental or comparative contexts include **DPP4**, **LEF1**, **DKK1**, **APCDD1**, **AXIN2**, and **PDPN** (Phan et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024), as well as functionally significant molecules such as podoplanin (PDPN) and netrin-1 (NTN1) (Griffin et al., 2020).

## Location

### Anatomical and Tissue-Level Distribution

Papillary fibroblasts are primarily found in the upper papillary dermis, the most superficial portion of the dermal layer, immediately beneath the epidermis.

> "F1 cells are predominantly located in the superficial papillary dermis"
>
> — Supplementary Table S7

> "the papillary dermis is the superficial layer of the dermis that lies immediately below the epidermis [29]"
>
> — Wang et al. (2024)

Further evidence from multiple sources, including single-cell analyses of human and pig skin, and organoid systems, supports this spatial localization and marker expression profile:

> "Skin organoids were rich in vimentin + dermal fibroblasts, which, much like mid-gestational fetal skin, clustered to FAP high papillary fibroblasts in the upper dermis and CD90 high FAP low reticular fibroblasts in the lower dermis (Figures 1E and S1D) (Korosec et al., 2019)."
>
> — Ramovs et al. (2022)

### Developmental Context

During early skin development, papillary fibroblasts emerge from upper dermal progenitors and contribute to specialized structures such as the dermal papilla and sheath (Phan et al., 2023):

> "Upper DFPs are shown to differentiate into papillary fibroblasts (clusters 7, 8, 16, 21), dermal papillae (clusters 4, 12, 20), dermal sheath (clusters 10), and mature fibroblasts (clusters 3, 17),"
>
> — Phan et al. (2023)

However, the strict anatomical separation between papillary and reticular fibroblasts is not absolute; some spatial and molecular gradients exist:

> "papillary and reticular fibroblasts are not completely separated according to their spatial location"
>
> — Liu et al. (2019)

## Function

### Extracellular Matrix Organization

F1 cells are strongly associated with ECM production and organization, reflecting their fundamental role in maintaining dermal structure and facilitating repair.

"Gene ontology enrichment for F1 papillary fibroblasts includes extracellular matrix organization." (Supplementary Table S9)

> "papillary fibroblast has enhanced collagen type VII and type III expressions related to papillary dermis formation, blood vessels, and dermo-epidermal junction (DEJ)"
>
> — Salazar et al. (2022)

> "the fibroblasts were closely related to [...] 'connective tissue development,' reflecting the role of dermal fibroblasts in generating the extracellular matrix and forming the connective tissue that maintains skin morphology and homeostasis (Figure 5F)."
>
> — Wang et al. (2024)

### Epidermal-Epidermal Interaction and Support

Papillary fibroblasts influence overlying epidermal behavior, including promoting the differentiation and maturation of keratinocytes:

> "Functionally, papillary fibroblasts have enhanced epidermal differentiation and maturation and secrete different soluble factors in reconstituted skin models [90] when compared with reticular fibroblasts."
>
> — Thangapazham et al. (2014)

### Hair Follicle Development and Regeneration

Papillary fibroblasts are the primary source for specialized cells involved in hair follicle formation and regeneration, including dermal papilla and arrector pili cells:

> "Fibroblasts that support hair follicle development differentiate from the papillary fibroblast lineage, into dermal papilla (DP), dermal sheath (DS), and arrector pili (AP) cells (Driskell et al., 2013;Plikus et al., 2017)."
>
> — Phan et al. (2020)

> "We have previously shown that papillary fibroblasts are the primary source of de-novo dermal papilla during skin development, which are required for hair formation (Driskell et al., 2013)."
>
> — Phan et al. (2020)

### Wound Healing and Regenerative Capacity

Papillary fibroblasts exhibit properties relevant to non-scarring (regenerative) wound healing, largely due to their secretory phenotype and pro-epidermal effects:

> "The fetal like gingival papillary fibroblasts may thus contribute to migration beneficial non-scarring wound healing in the oral mucosa."
>
> — Archana et al. (2014)

Reactivate expression of developmental transcription factors like Lef1 may enhance regenerative potential in adult wounds:

> "Our data suggests that activating the papillary region to retain its identity by expression of Lef1 has the potential to enhance wound healing in humans."
>
> — Phan et al. (2020)

### Proliferation and Differentiation Potential

Papillary fibroblasts are highly proliferative but lack adipogenic differentiation capacity, distinguishing them from reticular fibroblasts:

> "papillary fibroblasts have increased proliferative potential, express podoplanin (PDPN) and netrin-1 (NTN1) and do not differentiate into adipocytes (Korosec et al., 2019)"
>
> — Griffin et al. (2020)

## Structure / Morphology

No direct morphological evidence specific to F1 was found in the traversed literature. However, functional and spatial context implies a role in forming a fine, supportive meshwork within the superficial dermis, supporting the epidermis and appendageal structures.
